# Embedding Cryptographic Signatures in W3C PROV Documents: A Decision Report for inflexa / tsprov

## TL;DR
- **Keep your sidecar `.sig.json` as the verification artifact, but reshape it into a JCS-canonicalized, DSSE-style envelope rather than ad-hoc fields** — that gives you a self-contained, industry-aligned format without fighting PROV's lack of a canonical JSON form. If you want a single self-contained file, wrap envelope-bytes *around* the PROV-JSON rather than scattering signature attributes into the PROV graph.
- **The bundle-wrapping "signature entity wasGeneratedBy a signing activity" pattern is PROV-legitimate and has real conceptual precedent (Moreau's 2017 canonical-form work; PROV's "provenance of provenance" via `prov:Bundle`), but it is NOT a named, standardized pattern and it forces you to solve PROV-JSON's deterministic-serialization problem yourself** — exactly the hard part the rest of the industry avoids by enveloping.
- **The industry consensus for signed provenance is "envelope," not "embed" or pure "detached"**: in-toto/SLSA/Sigstore all use DSSE, which base64-wraps the payload as an opaque blob *specifically to avoid canonicalization*. Embedding-in-document (W3C VC Data Integrity, C2PA) exists but only pays off when the signed object must remain a live, queryable graph or travel inside a media file — which your threat model ("detect tampering after the fact," local CLI, no re-import of modified docs) does not require.

## Key Findings

**1. PROV specs are silent on signatures.** None of PROV-DM, PROV-O, PROV-N, PROV-CONSTRAINTS, or PROV-JSON define digital signatures or a document-integrity mechanism. The only place the PROV family touches integrity is **PROV-AQ (Provenance Access and Query, W3C Working Group Note, 30 April 2013)**, and only as advice, not mechanism: its Security Considerations state that when retrieving provenance, "steps SHOULD be taken to ensure the document itself is an accurate copy of the original whose author is being trusted (e.g. signature checking, or use of a trusted secure web service)." PROV-AQ delegates integrity/authenticity to transport-layer and out-of-band means (HTTPS, trusted services) and defines no signature format. It also notes that provenance "itself may be a valuable target for attack by malicious agents, and care must be taken to ensure it is" protected.

**2. The bundle pattern is real and PROV-native — but unnamed.** `prov:Bundle` is defined in PROV-DM Component 4 as "a named set of provenance descriptions, and is itself an entity, so allowing **provenance of provenance** to be expressed." PROV-O reinforces this: because a bundle is a kind of entity, all provenance assertions that can be made about entities can also be made about bundles, which "enables the creation of provenance of provenance." So it is legal to assert in an outer document that a signature entity `wasGeneratedBy` a signing activity, `wasAttributedTo` a signing agent, with hash/signature/publicKey carried as `prov` attributes. This is sound modeling. The recognized terms are **"provenance of provenance"** and **"meta-provenance"** (the bundle mechanism) and **"signed provenance" / "secure provenance" / "provenance integrity"** (the crypto). However, the *specific composite* you describe (records-in-named-bundle + outer signature-entity-`wasGeneratedBy`-signing-activity-with-hash-attributes) **does not have a single recognized name** and is not standardized — it is a natural combination of individually-citable primitives.

**3. The strongest academic precedent: Moreau's canonical form.** Luc Moreau, *"A Canonical Form for PROV Documents and its Application to Equality, Signature, and Validation,"* **ACM Transactions on Internet Technology, Vol. 17, No. 4, Article 35, published 18 August 2017, DOI 10.1145/3032990** (CC BY 4.0; open copy at University of Southampton ePrints, eprints.soton.ac.uk/404305). This is the most concrete, citable precedent for *signing W3C PROV*. It defines a canonical (normalized) form for PROV so a signature is independent of serialization (PROV-N, PROV-XML, PROV-JSON, RDF). Moreau writes, verbatim: "A signature of a PROV document allows the integrity and the author of provenance to be ascertained; since the signature is based on the canonical form, these checks are not tied to a particular encoding, but can be performed on any representation of PROV." Moreau states a signature "could be embedded inside a PROV document to form a signed PROV document," but explicitly leaves to a future standards group "the representation of the signature, and where it is allowed to be inserted." **That standardization never happened** — there is no W3C signature-embedding profile for PROV. Earlier secure-provenance lineage Moreau builds on: Hasan, Sion & Winslett, "The Case of the Fake Picasso" (USENIX FAST'09); Aldeco-Perez & Moreau, "Securing Provenance-based Audits" (IPAW'10, LNCS 6378); and Carroll, "Signing RDF Graphs" (ISWC'03).

**4. The VC-on-provenance precedent references rather than embeds the graph.** Barclay, Radha, Preece, Taylor & Nabrzyski (Cardiff University and University of Notre Dame), *"Certifying Provenance of Scientific Datasets with Self-sovereign Identity and Verifiable Credentials"* (12th International Workshop on Science Gateways, IWSG 2020; **arXiv:2004.02796**) applied W3C Verifiable Credentials JSON-LD proofs to dataset provenance using **Digital Bazaar's `vc-js`** + the `did:web` method + Node/Express. Importantly, **they did not embed a PROV graph in the credential** — the VC carried a *cryptographic hash of the dataset plus attribute claims* (e.g., `"Hash of Data": "0xFFEE...AA1122"`) and referenced the subject by DID. This confirms a real-world pattern of binding by hash + reference rather than embedding the whole provenance graph in the signed object.

**5. The deterministic-serialization problem is the crux.** To sign by hashing a serialization and verify by re-serializing, you need byte-stability. **PROV-JSON has no canonical form.** The W3C Member Submission (PROV-JSON, University of Southampton, 2013) structures a document as maps keyed by element type (`entity`, `activity`, `agent`, `<relationName>`) and by ID — JSON object key order is undefined, blank-node IDs "MUST be created... internally unique to a PROV-JSON serialization" (i.e., generated, not stable), and PROV literal values use a `{"$": value, "type": ...}` structure. Nothing guarantees two serializations of the same graph are byte-identical. The mainstream solutions:
   - **(a) JCS (RFC 8785, JSON Canonicalization Scheme).** Per RFC 8785 §3.2.3, "Property name strings to be sorted are formatted as arrays of UTF-16 [UNICODE] code units. The sorting is based on pure value comparisons, where code units are treated as unsigned integers, independent of locale settings." Whitespace is stripped and numbers normalized to ECMAScript/I-JSON form. Mature TS implementations: `canonicalize` (npm, 0 dependencies, TS types included), `json-canonicalize`, `@truestamp/canonify`. **Number caveat (verbatim RFC 8785):** "JSON number data MUST be expressible as IEEE 754 [IEEE754] double-precision values. For applications needing higher precision or longer integers than offered by IEEE 754 double precision, it is RECOMMENDED to represent such numbers as JSON strings." For PROV this is fine — timestamps and IDs are strings — but store any high-precision numeric as a string.
   - **(b) Sign the serialized bytes verbatim as an opaque blob** (the in-toto/DSSE approach) — base64 the exact bytes, sign those, never re-serialize. The PyPA "Index hosted attestations" spec states it plainly: "the attestation statement is encoded as a v1 in-toto Statement object, in JSON form. When serialized the statement is treated as an opaque binary blob, avoiding the need for canonicalization."
   - **(c) Content-address the semantic content** (Moreau canonical form, or hashing PROV-N) — robust but requires a canonicalizer you'd have to build.

**6. Industry consensus = envelope (DSSE), not embed.** in-toto's **DSSE (Dead Simple Signing Envelope)** was created precisely because "the legacy TUF/in-toto signature scheme" was "JSON-specific, relies on canonicalization." DSSE base64-wraps the payload with a `payloadType` and signs over a **PAE (Pre-Authentication Encoding)**, defined verbatim in secure-systems-lab/dsse protocol.md v1.0.0 as: `PAE(type, body) = "DSSEv1" + SP + LEN(type) + SP + type + SP + LEN(body) + SP + body` (where SP is the ASCII space, 0x20). **SLSA** provenance is an in-toto Statement wrapped in DSSE. **Sigstore/cosign** signs attestations as DSSE envelopes and wraps them in a Sigstore Bundle (adding cert/transparency-log material). **C2PA** (content provenance for media) *does* embed signed manifests into files using X.509 + COSE-style signatures — an *embedded* model — but that is driven by the need to travel inside a media file and bind to pixel content via a content hash, a different constraint than yours. **IPFS/Arweave** content-addressing makes the hash the identifier, giving integrity (any change → new CID) but **not authenticity** (no signer identity) — so it does not obviate a signature when you care about *who* signed.

**7. Detached vs enveloped vs embedded — the canonical taxonomy.** From XML-DSig and reused everywhere: **enveloped** (signature lives inside the payload), **enveloping** (signature wraps/contains the payload), **detached** (signature is a separate artifact). JWS supports detached/unencoded payloads (RFC 7797); COSE_Sign1 is the CBOR single-signer analog (and supports detached payload via a nil placeholder). Your current sidecar is **detached**. The bundle-embedding idea is **enveloped**. DSSE is **enveloping**.

**8. Crypto stack is well-supported on Bun.** Bun's WebCrypto (`crypto.subtle`) lists `Ed25519` among supported algorithms for `generateKey`/`sign`/`verify`, and SHA-256 via `digest`, so your "no external dependencies" goal is achievable. If you ever hit a runtime gap, `@noble/ed25519` (npm v3.1.0, June 2026) reports **0 dependencies** and **~3.9KB gzipped**, is "EDDSA signatures compliant with RFC8032, FIPS 186-5" with "SUF-CMA ... and SBS (non-repudiation / exclusive ownership)," and the noble project reports six audits by 2026 (including a Cure53 audit funded by OpenSats). JCS, if adopted, needs one small dependency (`canonicalize`, 0 transitive deps) or a ~30-line inline implementation.

## Details

### Should you embed via bundle, use DSSE, or keep the sidecar?

**The bundle-embedding approach is sound PROV modeling but solves the wrong problem and creates a new one.** It is legitimate to represent the signing event in PROV: an outer document containing the records-bundle as a `prov:Entity`, a `prov:Activity` (the signing), a `prov:Agent` (the signer/public key), `wasGeneratedBy`/`wasAttributedTo` relations, and the hash+signature+publicKey as attributes on a signature entity. This is precisely the "provenance of provenance" idiom and is the most PROV-idiomatic way to *describe* a signature. **But two problems:**

1. **You must define what bytes you signed.** If the signature attribute sits *inside* the same PROV-JSON document it signs, you hit the classic enveloped-signature chicken-and-egg: you must specify a canonicalization that excludes the signature attribute, re-serialize deterministically, and hash. PROV-JSON gives you no canonical form, so you own that spec forever. This is the exact trap DSSE was designed to avoid.
2. **It can break standard PROV tooling consumption.** Adding signing entities/activities/attributes into the graph means any downstream PROV consumer (ProvToolbox, the Python `prov` library, graph visualizers) will ingest your signature bookkeeping as if it were domain provenance — polluting queries, graphs, and validation. A `prov:Bundle` is valid PROV so it parses, but you have changed the semantic content every consumer sees. A detached/sidecar signature leaves the PROV file byte-for-byte untouched and fully standard.

**DSSE is the better fit if you want a single self-contained, independently-verifiable file.** Wrap your PROV-JSON as the opaque payload:
```json
{
  "payload": "<base64(PROV-JSON bytes, verbatim)>",
  "payloadType": "application/json; profile=prov-json",
  "signatures": [{ "keyid": "<ed25519 pubkey id>", "sig": "<base64(Ed25519 over PAE)>" }]
}
```
This gives you: (a) no canonicalization needed — you sign the exact bytes you stored and verify against the exact bytes you extract; (b) alignment with in-toto/SLSA/Sigstore tooling and mental models; (c) the public key travels with the artifact; (d) the wrapped PROV-JSON, once base64-decoded, is byte-identical and consumable by any standard PROV tool. The cost: the file is no longer *itself* a PROV document — a PROV tool must decode the envelope first. Given that you explicitly do **not** need to re-import externally-modified PROV and you want tamper-evidence, this trade is favorable.

**Keeping the sidecar is entirely defensible and arguably best for your threat model.** Your threat model is "detect tampering after the fact" on a local CLI, not preventing a malicious server. A detached signature over the stored PROV-JSON bytes achieves exactly this. What you *lose* by not embedding:
- **Coupling/portability:** the `.sig.json` can be separated, lost, or mismatched from its PROV file (no intrinsic 1:1 binding beyond filename convention). An envelope or embed keeps them inseparable.
- **Single-artifact ergonomics:** consumers must know to fetch and check two files.
- **Self-description:** a bare `{chainHash, signature, publicKey}` sidecar doesn't declare the algorithm, payload type, or *what bytes* were signed — making independent verification by a third party harder.

You can close most of that gap *without* embedding by upgrading the sidecar to a self-describing, DSSE-shaped envelope and signing over a **JCS-canonicalized** form of the PROV-JSON (so verification is robust even if the PROV file is re-serialized by a different tool).

### What bytes to sign — concrete recommendation
Sign over **JCS(PROV-JSON)** if you want verification to survive re-serialization (pretty-print vs minified, different key order), OR sign over the **verbatim stored bytes** (DSSE-style) if you control both write and read and never re-serialize. For a local CLI that writes the file once and never re-imports a modified one, **verbatim-bytes signing is simplest and eliminates an entire class of canonicalization bugs.** Use JCS only if you anticipate the PROV-JSON being reformatted between signing and verification, or a non-TS verifier.

### Why not full Linked Data Proofs / VC Data Integrity?
W3C VC Data Integrity embeds a `proof` block directly in JSON-LD and supports `Ed25519Signature2020`. Libraries: `@digitalbazaar/jsonld-signatures`, `@transmute/linked-data-proof`, `vc-js`. **But:** (1) PROV-JSON is *not* JSON-LD — it has no `@context`; applying LD Proofs would require migrating to PROV-O JSON-LD (or the 2024 PROV-JSONLD Member Submission) and pulling in RDF Dataset Canonicalization (URDNA2015 / RDFC-1.0), a heavy dependency. (2) LD Proofs' canonicalization is RDF-graph canonicalization, far more complex than JCS. (3) Their selective-disclosure/BBS+ features are irrelevant to your "detect tampering" goal. This is the heaviest path and buys you nothing your threat model needs. The simpler path that avoids the full LD-Proofs machinery is exactly JCS-or-verbatim + Ed25519, which you already have.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Adopt now (low effort, high payoff):** Keep the detached sidecar but **reshape it into a self-describing DSSE-style envelope** and define explicitly which bytes are signed. Minimum fields: `payloadType`, the signing algorithm (`Ed25519` over `SHA-256`), `chainHash`, `sig`, `publicKey`/`keyid`, and a `payloadDigestMethod` declaring `verbatim` or `JCS`. This is a small change to your existing column-to-file export and makes the artifact independently verifiable by anyone with the public key.

**Stage 2 — If/when you need a single self-contained artifact:** Emit a DSSE envelope with the PROV-JSON as the base64 `payload`, signed over the DSSE PAE. Do **not** scatter signature attributes into the PROV graph. This keeps the inner PROV-JSON pristine and standard-tool-consumable after one base64 decode, and aligns you with in-toto/SLSA conventions your users may already recognize.

**Stage 3 — Only if you have a hard requirement that the signed file itself be a queryable PROV document** (e.g., a downstream system ingests it directly into a PROV store and must see the signing event as provenance): use the bundle pattern (records in a named `prov:Bundle`; outer signature entity `wasGeneratedBy` a signing activity), **and** publish a precise canonicalization spec (recommend JCS over the bundle's serialization with the signature attribute excluded). Treat this as a real standards-authoring commitment, citing Moreau 2017 as the conceptual basis.

**Decision thresholds that would change the recommendation:**
- If you ever need to **distribute provenance over the web with signer identity verified by third parties at scale** → move toward Sigstore-style bundles (DSSE + transparency log).
- If the provenance must **travel embedded inside another file format** (like media) → look at C2PA/COSE embedded manifests.
- If you adopt **PROV-O JSON-LD** for semantic-web interop anyway → then (and only then) VC Data Integrity / LD Proofs become a natural fit.
- If a **non-TS consumer must verify** → prefer **JCS** (RFC 8785, multi-language) over verbatim-byte signing, and document the canonicalization precisely.

## Caveats
- **Moreau 2017 proposes embedding but no W3C profile ever standardized *where* a signature goes in a PROV document.** Any embed/bundle scheme you build is bespoke; you cannot claim interoperability with other PROV signers because none is standardized.
- **PROV-JSON's lack of a canonical form is a genuine, unsolved standards gap** — the Member Submission defines structure but not byte-determinism, and blank-node IDs are generated by the serializer. Do not assume two tools produce identical PROV-JSON bytes.
- **JCS has known sharp edges:** it normalizes numbers via IEEE-754 doubles (store precise numbers as strings, per RFC 8785) and rejects `NaN`/`Infinity`; some commentators consider its ECMAScript number dependence fragile across languages. For a single TS/Bun codebase this is a non-issue.
- **Content-addressing (IPFS/Arweave) gives integrity, not authenticity** — a CID proves the bytes are unchanged but says nothing about *who* produced them, so it does not replace your Ed25519 signature.
- **`keyid` in DSSE/in-toto is an untrusted hint, not a trust anchor** — the in-toto/SLSA documentation warns the key ID "cannot be trusted! Its main purpose is to allow the verifier to choose the relevant public key more easily." Trust in the public key must come from your own key-management/pinning, not from the envelope.
- **Bun's Ed25519 WebCrypto support has had historical bugs** (an earlier sign/verify round-trip defect was reported and patched in oven-sh/bun); pin a known-good Bun version and add a self-test, or keep `@noble/ed25519` as a fallback.
- The exact "signature entity `wasGeneratedBy` signing activity carrying hash attributes" pattern, while modeled correctly, **is not a recognized named pattern** — don't present it to stakeholders as an established standard.
